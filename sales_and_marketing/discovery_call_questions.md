# Discovery Call Questions

## Overview

This document provides a structured framework for conducting discovery calls with potential aquaculture investment clients. Effective discovery ensures we understand client needs, qualify opportunities, and position solutions appropriately.

---

## Discovery Call Structure

### Call Duration and Format

**Typical Duration:** 30-60 minutes

**Format:**
1. Introduction and rapport (5 min)
2. Qualification questions (10-15 min)
3. Needs assessment (15-20 min)
4. Initial solution discussion (10-15 min)
5. Next steps and close (5 min)

---

## Qualification Questions

### Investment Capacity

**Purpose:** Determine if client has financial resources for the project scope.

---

**Q1: What is the investment range you're considering for this aquaculture project?**

*Listen for:*
- Specific budget range
- Funding source (equity, debt, mixed)
- Comfort level with investment size

*Follow-up if vague:*
- "Are you thinking of a smaller pilot project or full commercial scale?"
- "What range would be comfortable for you: SAR 10-20 million, 20-50 million, or larger?"

*Red flags:*
- No budget consideration
- Unrealistic expectations (high production, low investment)
- Funding not secured

---

**Q2: Have you secured financing or investment capital for this project?**

*Listen for:*
- Committed capital vs. exploring
- ADF loan interest or approval
- Other investors involved

*Follow-up:*
- "Are you familiar with Agricultural Development Fund (ADF) financing options?"
- "Would you be interested in learning about typical funding structures for aquaculture projects?"

---

**Q3: What is your expected return requirement for this investment?**

*Listen for:*
- IRR expectations (realistic: 15-25%)
- Payback period expectations
- Understanding of aquaculture economics

*Follow-up if unrealistic:*
- "Based on our experience, commercial RAS projects typically achieve 15-25% IRR. Does that align with your expectations?"

---

### Timeline

**Purpose:** Understand urgency and decision timeline.

---

**Q4: What is your target timeline for having operations up and running?**

*Listen for:*
- Specific date or event driving timeline
- Urgency level
- Understanding of project duration

*Reality check:*
- Typical project: 18-36 months from start to production
- Feasibility + Design: 4-6 months
- Permits + Construction: 12-18 months
- Commissioning + Ramp-up: 6-12 months

---

**Q5: What is driving this timeline?**

*Listen for:*
- Market opportunity
- Government incentives
- Investment fund requirements
- Personal goals

---

**Q6: When do you need to make a decision on moving forward?**

*Listen for:*
- Decision timeline
- Dependencies (board approval, partner agreement)
- Urgency

---

### Authority

**Purpose:** Identify decision makers and buying process.

---

**Q7: Who else will be involved in the decision to proceed with this project?**

*Listen for:*
- Other stakeholders
- Family members
- Business partners
- Advisors

*Follow-up:*
- "Would it be helpful to involve them in our next discussion?"
- "What is their role in the decision process?"

---

**Q8: What does your approval process look like for an investment of this size?**

*Listen for:*
- Formal process or individual decision
- Board/committee involvement
- Required documentation

---

**Q9: Have you worked with external consultants or service providers for similar projects before?**

*Listen for:*
- Experience with consulting engagements
- Expectations for process
- Past positive/negative experiences

---

### Experience

**Purpose:** Understand client's background and capability.

---

**Q10: What is your experience with aquaculture or related industries?**

*Listen for:*
- Direct aquaculture experience
- Agriculture/farming experience
- Food/seafood industry knowledge
- Complete newcomer

*Implications:*
- Experienced: Faster ramp-up, focused discussions
- Newcomer: More education needed, full-service likely required

---

**Q11: Do you have a team in place for this project, or will you need to build one?**

*Listen for:*
- Existing team capabilities
- Hiring plans
- Openness to outsourced management

---

## Needs Assessment Questions

### Production Goals

**Purpose:** Understand vision and scope for the project.

---

**Q12: What production volume are you targeting? (tons per year)**

*Listen for:*
- Specific target or range
- Understanding of scale
- Ambition level

*Guide if unsure:*
- Small: 50-100 tons/year (entry level)
- Medium: 200-500 tons/year (commercial)
- Large: 500-1,000+ tons/year (industrial)

---

**Q13: What species are you interested in producing?**

*Listen for:*
- Specific species (tilapia, barramundi, shrimp)
- Market-driven vs. preference-driven
- Flexibility

*Follow-up:*
- "What is driving your interest in [species]?"
- "Have you considered [alternative species] based on market demand?"

---

**Q14: Who will you sell to? What market channels are you considering?**

*Listen for:*
- Domestic vs. export
- Wholesale, retail, food service
- Existing relationships
- Market knowledge

*Follow-up:*
- "Do you have existing relationships with buyers?"
- "Have you researched pricing in these channels?"

---

### Site and Location

**Purpose:** Assess site availability and suitability.

---

**Q15: Have you identified a site for this project?**

*Listen for:*
- Site secured vs. searching
- Location details
- Land ownership status

*Follow-up if site not identified:*
- "Do you have preferences for location?"
- "Would you like assistance in site identification and evaluation?"

---

**Q16: What do you know about utility availability at the site? (Power, water, access)**

*Listen for:*
- Electrical capacity
- Water source
- Road access
- Regulatory zoning

---

**Q17: What is the land size and configuration?**

*Listen for:*
- Available area (m²)
- Constraints (shape, terrain)
- Future expansion potential

---

### Capabilities and Support

**Purpose:** Identify capability gaps and service needs.

---

**Q18: What aspects of the project do you feel confident handling internally?**

*Listen for:*
- Self-assessment of capabilities
- Areas of expertise
- Willingness to learn

---

**Q19: Where do you feel you need the most support?**

*Listen for:*
- Technical design
- Project management
- Operations
- Market access
- Financing

*Common needs:*
- Newcomers: Everything
- Experienced: Specific technical or operational gaps

---

**Q20: Are you looking for turnkey solutions, or do you prefer to manage components yourself?**

*Listen for:*
- Level of involvement desired
- Passive investor vs. active operator
- Management bandwidth

---

**Q21: What is your vision for ongoing operations – do you plan to operate in-house or outsource management?**

*Listen for:*
- In-house capability plans
- Interest in operations services
- Long-term involvement

## Objection Handling

### Common Objections and Responses

---

**Objection: "The investment seems too high."**

**Response Framework:**
1. Acknowledge the concern
2. Put in context (cost per ton capacity, industry benchmarks)
3. Highlight returns and payback
4. Offer phased approach if appropriate

*Example:*
> "I understand the investment is significant. Let me share some context: a well-designed 200-ton facility typically runs SAR 50-60 million, which translates to SAR 250-300 per kg annual capacity. With proper execution, these projects achieve 18-25% IRR and 5-7 year payback. We can also explore phased approaches to manage initial outlay."

---

**Objection: "I'm not sure about the technology risk."**

**Response Framework:**
1. Acknowledge RAS is specialized
2. Emphasize proven technology
3. Share track record and references
4. Highlight our expertise in reducing risk

*Example:*
> "RAS is indeed a specialized technology, but it's far from experimental. RAS has been commercially proven for decades in Europe and is now scaling globally. Our approach uses proven equipment from established suppliers, and we bring hands-on operational experience to ensure proper commissioning and ramp-up."

---

**Objection: "The timeline is too long."**

**Response Framework:**
1. Explain timeline components
2. Highlight critical path items
3. Discuss acceleration options where possible
4. Set realistic expectations

*Example:*
> "I understand you want to move quickly. Let me break down the timeline: permitting typically takes 3-4 months, design 2-3 months, construction 8-12 months, and commissioning 2-3 months. We can run some phases in parallel and prioritize long-lead items to compress the schedule. However, rushing critical steps often leads to costly problems. Our goal is fastest path to reliable production."

---

**Objection: "I want to do this myself / don't need consulting."**

**Response Framework:**
1. Acknowledge entrepreneurial spirit
2. Highlight specific risks of DIY approach
3. Offer focused engagement options
4. Leave door open for future support

*Example:*
> "I respect your confidence and entrepreneurial approach. Many successful operators started that way. What I'd recommend is at least a focused feasibility assessment to validate your assumptions before committing full capital. We've seen projects fail due to design flaws or market miscalculations that could have been avoided with proper due diligence. We can structure a limited engagement that gives you validation without a large commitment."

---

**Objection: "Imported fish is cheaper."**

**Response Framework:**
1. Acknowledge import competition
2. Highlight fresh/local advantages
3. Discuss premium market segments
4. Reference Vision 2030 trends

*Example:*
> "You're right that imported frozen fish can be cheaper. However, we see local RAS production winning in several areas: fresh premium markets (restaurants, hotels) that pay 20-40% premiums, reduced logistics costs and spoilage, supply reliability, and growing consumer preference for local sustainable products. Vision 2030 is also creating tailwinds for domestic food production. Our projects are designed to compete profitably in these segments."

---

**Objection: "I want to start small and see how it goes."**

**Response Framework:**
1. Validate the cautious approach
2. Explain economies of scale
3. Propose viable minimum scale
4. Discuss expansion planning

*Example:*
> "A cautious approach makes sense, especially for new entrants. However, I should mention that aquaculture economics improve significantly with scale. A 50-ton facility has higher per-kg costs than 200 tons. The minimum viable commercial scale is typically 100-150 tons. We can design for expansion – start at 100 tons with infrastructure sized for 200 – so you can grow once proven. This balances risk with economics."

---

**Objection: "I need to think about it."**

**Response Framework:**
1. Acknowledge need for consideration
2. Understand specific concerns
3. Offer additional information
4. Propose specific next step and timeline

*Example:*
> "Absolutely, this is a significant decision that deserves careful thought. Can I ask what specific aspects you're weighing? Perhaps there's additional information I can provide to help your evaluation. Would it be helpful if I sent you a summary document and we scheduled a follow-up call next week to address any questions?"

## Discovery Call Best Practices

### Before the Call

- [ ] Research the prospect (company, individual, industry)
- [ ] Review any prior communications
- [ ] Prepare relevant case studies or references
- [ ] Have presentation materials ready if needed
- [ ] Test technology for video calls
- [ ] Block adequate time without interruptions

### During the Call

- [ ] Start with relationship building
- [ ] Listen more than talk (aim for 70/30)
- [ ] Take notes on key points
- [ ] Ask clarifying questions
- [ ] Summarize understanding periodically
- [ ] Avoid jumping to solutions too quickly
- [ ] Confirm understanding before closing

### After the Call

- [ ] Send summary email within 24 hours
- [ ] Document in CRM/tracking system
- [ ] Assign internal follow-up actions
- [ ] Schedule next meeting
- [ ] Prepare requested materials
- [ ] Update opportunity status and qualification
